# RFBA Research Hypothesis
## Working Hypothesis

A fusion reactor boundary designed as a regenerative and adaptive system—incorporating renewable or sacrificial protective layers, circulating protective media, continuous damage sensing, and AI-assisted control—may achieve greater operational lifetime and availability than conventional static boundary architectures.

Rather than attempting to create materials that resist degradation indefinitely, this research investigates whether degradation can be expected, detected, managed, and continuously mitigated while maintaining overall reactor operation.

## Biological Inspiration

Biological organisms maintain long-term system functionality even though individual components continuously degrade and are replaced.

Examples include:

- Skin continuously renewing itself
- Blood circulating heat, nutrients, and waste
- Bones undergoing continuous remodeling
- Biological systems detecting and responding to damage
- Multiple protective layers surrounding critical organs
- Homeostasis maintaining stable internal conditions

RFBA investigates whether similar design principles can be applied conceptually to fusion reactor boundary systems.

## Primary Research Question

Can a dynamically managed and regenerative fusion reactor boundary achieve greater operational availability and useful lifetime than an equivalent boundary based primarily on static, long-lived materials?

## Current Status

This is an early-stage research hypothesis.

The concept has not been experimentally validated, and no claim of scientific novelty is currently being made.

The purpose of the RFBA research project is to investigate existing scientific literature and prior art, identify potential research gaps, develop computational models, and determine whether the hypothesis is scientifically and engineeringly viable.
